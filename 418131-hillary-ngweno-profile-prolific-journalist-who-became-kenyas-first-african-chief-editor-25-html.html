Ng'weno died peacefully at home in the bosom of his lovely family just 10 days after celebrating his 83rd birthday.

Early life and education

The renowned scribe was born on June 28, 1938, in Nairobi to the late Morris Onyango.

The gallant journalist was raised up in Muthurwa where his father worked for the East African Railways.

He began his primary school education at Saint Peter Cleavers.

After posting exemplary performance, he was offered a chance at Mang'u High School, one of Kenya's top-performing national schools.

From Mang'u, he joined the United States of America based Harvard University where he studied Physics and Mathematics.

Illustrious career in journalism

Despite his science-oriented studies, it was at Harvard University that Ng’weno, established, shaped and sharpened his celebrated and envied journalism career.

In 1960 while still a student, he began writing cyclostyled newsletters.

He returned to Kenya in 1962 and joined the Nation Media Group (NMG), where he became a reporter, feature writer and columnist at the Daily Nation newspaper.

It appears that he met Aga Khan, the NMG founder, while still at Harvard, who gave him an opportunity at his then young company.

In 1965, at the age of 25, he became the first African editor-in-chief of the Daily Nation, succeeding Graham Rees.

However, he resigned after working for few years over editorial differences but maintained a good working relationship with NMG.

Own newspapers

In the early 1970s, Ng'weno launched Joe Magazine, a must-read outlet due to its blend of political satire, cartoon and humour.

In 1975, he founded the Weekly Review, a groundbreaking magazine the for the first time had political and economic analysis for Kenyan readers.

The revered journalist also established the Nairobi Times, a modelled form of the established New York Times and Times of London.

The outlet was, however, reduced to a tabloid and was sold to Kenya African National Union (KANU), which rebranded it to Kenya Times.

STV launch

In 1986, the late came up with Usiniharakishe, a television series that aired on the state-owned Kenya Broadcasting Cooperation (KBC).

However, the show was was banned after airing only two episodes but he replaced it with Tushauriane.

He then went ahead and launched the first-ever local independent TV, STV which ended KBC's monopoly.

STV was later bought by the now Mediamax company which is associated with President Uhuru Kenyatta.

The famous weekly review folded in 2001, but while in retirement, he continued to produce, write and direct a series of documentaries under the NMG.

He has since donated his invaluable archive Center for East Africa Media Research at Moi University, which bears his name.

He also has a YouTube channel that streams his documentaries.

Marriage and family

While in the USA, Ng'weno married a France native, Fleur Arabelle Grandjouan, now Fleur Ng’weno.

Fleur is a Bachelor of Science in conservation holder from the University of Michigan and a former editor of the Rainbow magazine.

She has been actively involved in environmental issues in Kenya for more than forty years, serving as Honorary Secretary to NatureKenya.

Before his demise, the two had been married for close to fifty years and were blessed with two daughters, Amolo Ng'weno and Bettina Ng'weno.

Amolo is also a Harvard and Princeton educated. She served as deputy director of financial services for the poor at the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation.

On the other hand, Bettina is an associate professor of African American and African studies at the University of California.
